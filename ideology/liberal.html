LIBERALISM – The Ideology of Reform

Liberalism is one of the six core political ideologies on the Map My Politics ideology chart, occupying the area to the left of the centrist zone. This is appropriate, in terms of the conventional left-right continuum, because liberalism – in the American sense of the ideology – is somewhat left of centrism economically and quite a bit left of conservatism. But liberals place roughly equal value on personal liberty, the second axis on the chart.

Regarding economic liberty, however, there is no doubt: liberals embrace state intervention in the free market in order to regulate capitalism’s perceived shortcomings. Those flaws include protection against anti-competitive behavior such as monopoly action, curtailing detrimental market fluctuations, as well as generating sufficient tax revenues to pay for beneficial government programs and to correct excessive disparities of wealth. The progressive or reform aspect of liberalism is on full display in the context of economic matters, as seen in a willingness to step in and “fix” those problems. It’s not that liberalism rejects the capitalist model outright; that would be incorrectly overstating the case. It’s rather that liberalism accepts capitalism as beneficial but also fundamentally imperfect in improving the general welfare or common good.

Regarding the y-axis of social liberty, modern liberals are fully supportive of a classically liberal defense of civil liberties, such as freedom of speech, press and religion. Such support might sometimes be viewed as more zealous than conservatives in matters of social policy, such as gender equality, owing to a conservative’s natural propensity towards upholding tradition and a liberal’s core propensity for progressive change. But liberals are not as uniformly devoted to personal liberty as their social libertarian neighbors below them on the Map My Politics chart, especially on matters like offensive free speech and unregulated gun ownership that conflict with other valued liberal principles. On a fundamental level, however, liberalism is very much in the mainstream of classical liberal thought when it comes to supporting social liberty. In doing so liberals differ from their populist neighbors to the north by placing the personal rights of the individual above the will of the majority, as expressed through the power of the state.

The roots of modern liberalism can be traced to post-Enlightenment philosophers who expanded on the classical Enlightenment notions of life, liberty and property by introducing a regard for social justice. Jeremy Bentham’s theory of utilitarianism – seeking the greatest good for the greatest number – was perhaps the most instrumental in this regard. And John Stuart Mill, the “Father of Social Liberalism,” built upon Bentham’s work by arguing that a government should establish social and economic policies that both protect individuals from the “tyranny of the majority” while also promoting equality of opportunity, thereby alleviating poverty. For Mill, such policies included income and estate taxes, and he was one of the first British Members of Parliament to openly support women’s suffrage and to advocate for the abolition of slavery in America. The 20th century American philosopher, John Rawls, was tremendously influential on liberal thought by positing a rigorous moral basis for social justice beyond majoritarian-based utility in his landmark work, A Theory of Justice.

The United States is – for the most part – a two-party system, and liberals have typically chosen to associate themselves with the more progressive of the two: the Democratic Party. Perhaps the archetypal American liberal politician was Franklin D. Roosevelt, who was propelled to historical prominence by the Great Depression, which he sought to alleviate with his New Deal legislation of the 1930s. FDR’s legacy cast a long liberal shadow into the middle and latter part of the 20th century and ushered in a modern version of federalism that Lyndon Johnson built upon with his Great Society legislation in the 1960s. Prominent American liberal politicians have more recently included Michael Dukakis, Shirley Chisholm, Walter Mondale, Elizabeth Warren, Nancy Pelosi and Kamala Harris. Important modern American liberal thinkers and writers have included Hendrick Hertzberg, Paul Krugman, Ta-Nehisi Coates and Rachel Maddow.
