POPULISM – The Anti-Establishment Ideology

Populism is one of the six core political ideologies on the Map My Politics ideology chart, occupying the area above the liberal, centrist and conservative zones. This is because populism encompasses both left-wing and right-wing beliefs in terms of the conventional left-right continuum of relative economic freedom. The placement of populism above the other core ideologies on the second or y-axis reflects populism’s disposition to elevate the will of the majority above protection of personal liberty.

Regarding economic liberty, populists run the gamut from a right-wing embrace of capitalism to a left-wing rejection of the free market in favor of redistribution of wealth. The common denominator among the various forms of populist economics is that the interests of the common people should come first, not those of the perceived elites. That’s why populism is fundamentally a grassroots, anti-establishment ideology that tends to flourish in times of economic upheaval and when the ruling establishment is identified as out of touch with the concerns of ordinary citizens. In this regard, it makes sense that populist sentiment can be relatively indifferent about the proposed methods of economic recovery, including whether corporate taxation or regulation should increase or not. What matters more is that the financial needs of everyday citizens are perceived as being met.

Populism is more defined by a willingness to forsake some civil liberties in order to advance certain majoritarian concerns, which explains its placement in the upper quadrants of the Map My Politics chart, as defined by the y-axis of relative social liberty. This predisposition can take the form of adherence to traditional or religious values, for example in rejecting legalized abortion or same-sex marriage. Alternatively, it might manifest itself in anti-immigration terms, either out of concern for diminishment of job opportunities or out of fear of multiculturism – or both. This ultimately reflects populism’s “voice of the people” democratic nature. Populism is more authoritarian than libertarian only in the sense that it values the democratic authority of the majority over the protection of minority interests. A populist doesn’t want to have his or her personal liberty restricted by the government any more than the next person, so populism is generally supportive of protections like freedom of religion and speech, just less so when they come into conflict with other values. What truly defines populism in this dimension is a rejection of liberties that are viewed as being imposed by perceived elites or as inconsistent with mainstream values.

The roots of populism can be traced to 19th century democratic reform movements, such as America’s agrarianism or Britain’s Luddites who rebelled against the industrialization of jobs. Because of populism’s majoritarian, anti-elitist nature, it doesn’t have the same philosophical forebears as the other Map My Politics core ideologies, no John Stuart Mill to champion modern liberalism or Edmund Burke for conservatism. Populism is intrinsically a politically-oriented ideology rather than an intellectually-based one, and typically arises nationally or regionally, so the closest one might find to a so-called “Father of Populism” in American political thought might be Thomas Jefferson, owing to Jefferson’s bedrock faith in democracy and the common man, hatred of corruption and his rejection of aristocratic elites. A more populist strain of Jeffersonian democracy took hold with the later ascent of Jacksonian democracy and the eventual growth of populist movements of such political figures as William Jennings Bryan and the La Follette family of Wisconsin.

The United States is – for the most part – a two-party system, and populists are found in both parties. The two mainstream parties have traditionally been administered hierarchically by established party leaders, however, so the dominant trend of third parties has been as vehicles to house populist candidacies or movements, such as the Populist or “Bull Moose” Party of Teddy Roosevelt, the later Populist Party of Robert La Follette, Sr., and the Reform Party of Ross Perot. Perhaps the definitive modern American populist politician was Huey Long, who served as both Governor of Louisiana and as a U.S. Senator. A Democrat, Long’s “Share Our Wealth” economic agenda was much to the left of even Franklin D. Roosevelt, who viewed Long as a dangerous demagogue despite Long’s occasional support of FDR’s New Deal legislation. Other prominent American populist politicians more recently include Ralph Nader, Bernie Sanders (also claimed by democratic socialists), Sarah Palin, and Donald Trump, and contemporary populist movements have included the Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street. Important modern American populist writers have included Jim Hightower, Molly Ivins, and Laura Ingraham.
